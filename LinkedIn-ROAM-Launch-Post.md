# LinkedIn Post — ROAM Project Launch

---

On March 31, nearly 100 Baidu Apollo robotaxis shut down simultaneously on Wuhan's elevated highways. Passengers were trapped for two hours. SOS buttons went unanswered. The only resolution? Police rescuing vehicles one by one.

Three months earlier, a power outage in San Francisco left Waymo's fleet stranded — emergency services called Waymo's hotline 31 times, waiting a combined 2.5 hours.

As robotaxis scale from pilot programs to city-wide operations, incidents like these will become more frequent, not less. Every operator — Waymo, Baidu, Pony.ai, Tesla — has built their own remote operations platform. But each follows a completely different philosophy. There is no shared baseline.

That's why I started ROAM — an open-source project to establish one.

ROAM provides four modules:

📋 An incident database with 16+ structured records across 4 operators
🏷️ A scenario taxonomy (6 categories, 27 sub-scenarios) with severity and urgency scales
🏗️ A three-layer reference architecture (AI autonomous → human confirm → remote driving)
📊 Evaluation benchmarks with 8 KPIs

Is it perfect? Far from it. It's a first attempt based on publicly available information, and there's much room for improvement. But I believe the industry needs a shared starting point — not for any single company, but for:

→ Robotaxi operators planning remote operations at scale
→ OEMs entering the L4 service market
→ Regulators developing safety frameworks
→ Future service providers who will manage urban robotaxi operations and incident response

The project is aligned with China's draft GB ADS safety standard (Appendix C.2), the UN WP29 GTR on ADS, and Germany's StVFernLV — the first teleoperation regulation in Europe.

I hope this can serve as a starting point. Contributions, critiques, and forks are all welcome.
